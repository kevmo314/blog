On Feedback Loops

October 15th, 2020

For early startups, growth is paramount to success. To drive growth, Andrew Chen advocates focusing on acquisition and engagement loops. For us at Huddle, this means we think a lot about how to create feedback loops to acquire and engage brands, consumers, and influencers. That's a lot of loops to nail down! And chasing positive feedback loops can be daunting: it's a vaguely-structured idea that's easy to tell when it exists but not so easy to create from scratch. Indeed, in Andrew's post, he covers many great examples of feedback loops, but actually creating them is left as an exercise for the reader.

Our approach to reliably and repeatedly creating positive feedback loops involves looking at a more microeconomic level with individual incentive alignment. We find our strongest alignment not by direct incentives such as taxes and subsidies, but rather by applying structural nudges to encourage the equilibria we want to reach.

1. Choose a motivation

Why did the user open our app? Users use a product for a whole slew of motivations. For Huddle, perhaps they're coming to shop or maybe they're seeking a new connection. Perhaps they received a notification and are reengaging. This motivation is the user's intent. To focus on growth, we choose a specific set of intents that we wish to encourage.

2. Map out the journey with this motivation

What will the user do in order to fulfill their motivation? Can we make it easier with fewer steps?

3. Verify that the motivation is satisfied

Did the user actually find what they're looking for? At Google, I found the best way to verify that users were indeed satisfied was just watching them use the app. No conversations, no questions, just observing.

Watching users makes it pretty clear when the UI isn't up to par. For example, Google flights will tell you when a flight is substantially delayed.

When this feature first launched, it was buried behind a tooltip when you hovered over an icon. Turns out, very few users actually discovered this tooltip, which became obvious when looking at someone use the website. Verifying that motivations are satisfied guarantees that engaged users will guide the product development flow instead of guessing.

Why we think this matters

We differentiate our strategy by focusing on individual motivations instead of the actions we want to encourage. Instead of just considering "why might a viewer want to view content?", we ask "why might a viewer want to view content given that the content creator is seeking new friends?". For us, it's important to pin the initial motivation and build the product around that motivation and user journey instead of individual components of the feedback loop.

When I was at Google, our growth strategies consisted of focusing on critical user journeys. I see aligning incentives to be an iteration of that strategy, focusing on user-defined motivations instead of product-defined journeys.

Incentives and emotions are core to human behavior. Arbitrary user journeys are not. Tying a user journey to addressing a core incentive, such as feeling the need for connection and socialization, grounds our user journey in a repeatable, exogenous trigger instead of a product-level decision.

Put simply, we change our mindset from "does this feature help users find the content they want?" to "does this features help users address the reason they came to the app?", which we believe both strengthens the feedback loop and simplifies the development. Huddle is still a young startup, so this approach has yet to be proven. But it's the lens we view everything through and drives our decision making all across the company, from product to hiring.
