IRL Streaming: Frequently Asked Questions

May 16th, 2021

People often ask about my setup and ask how they can get started streaming with it. If you're new to streaming, here's a simple FAQ for the most common questions I see about how to start streaming.

How do I stream?

Step 1: Click the stream button in the Twitch app.

Step 2: Click Stream IRL.

Step 3: Stream.

Tada, you're streaming!

How do I set up an RTMP server?

Don't bother, you don't need one.

How do I use SRT?

Doesn't matter. Just click stream.

Should I get a LiveU?

No.

How do I build my own LiveU?

Don't bother, use your phone.

How do I get 1080p 60fps?

Who the fuck cares.

How do I add overlays?

Dunno, figure it out later. Doesn't matter.

How do I stabilize the video?

Don't move your hand so much.

How many SIM cards do I need?

One: your phone's.

Which cell provider is the best?

All of them. Whichever you're paying for now is fine.

Won't I run out of data?

You probably haven't yet. Deal with that when you run out of data.

Do I have enough bandwidth?

Dunno, click that stream button and find out.

Why is this FAQ so unhelpful?

The biggest key to success is consistency. It's not content, it's not stream quality, it's not stability. Be consistent, observe the patterns, contribute to the community, and you will grow. Your viewers don't care if you have mediocre content, terrible quality, or unstable connections. They are there because they want to spend time with you.

I see a lot of new streamers get so caught up in their desire to have a perfect stream that they forget the whole goal in the first place: to stream. Put your content out there. It doesn't matter if it's bad, it doesn't matter if it's scuffed. Start with the lowest bar in the world and work up from there. Don't waste time and money finding solutions to a problem you don't even have. Many of the biggest IRL streamers use nothing but a cell phone and gimbal, but instead of wasting time crafting the perfect streaming platform, they're actually out there streaming and building their audience. That's why they're successful.

Until a problem is staring at you right in the face and it needs to be solved because your viewers are complaining about it, don't bother. For what it's worth, I've never heard of anyone not watching a stream because there are no overlays.

Unsurprisingly, this is true for a lot more than just streaming.
